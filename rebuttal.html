B2
Reframe from "Early Detection" to "Risk Screening"
Comment
The committee noted that claiming "early detection" may overclaim the contribution, since IWGDF Category 1 is defined by LOPS (monofilament) and PAD (ABI) — neurological and vascular conditions not directly observable in plantar pressure images. A Category 1 patient with LOPS but normal foot structure may show no abnormal pressure pattern.
Action Taken
The committee's concern is acknowledged as valid. The model detects only mechanical risk patterns visible in plantar pressure distribution (e.g. high forefoot pressure, deformity-related contact patterns) and cannot infer LOPS or PAD directly from the image. Framing has been revised throughout from "early detection" to "risk screening" — the tool screens for risk patterns in footprint images only and does not replace monofilament or ABI examination.

Regarding Presentation & Methodology Clarification
C1
Justify Choice of Statistical Significance Tests — McNemar's Test and DeLong's Test
Comment
During the examination, the candidate was unable to clearly justify the choice of McNemar's Test and DeLong's Test as the statistical significance tests used to compare model performance (Section 2.8.3; applied in RQ2 and RQ3). Please clarify why these two specific tests were selected, what each test evaluates, and why they are appropriate for this study.
Action Taken
1. Why a paired test design is required. Both comparisons in this study (S1 vs. S2 in RQ2, and the proposed CNN vs. the Baseline in RQ3) are evaluated on the exact same held-out test set. The two sets of predictions are therefore paired, not independent samples. Standard tests that assume independent groups would ignore the correlation between the two models' outputs on the same patients, which can produce an incorrect significance conclusion. McNemar's Test and DeLong's Test were selected because both are designed specifically for paired classifier comparisons on a shared test set, following Rainio et al. (2024).

2. McNemar's Test compares two classifiers at a fixed decision threshold (0.5). It isolates the discordant pairs — cases where the two models disagree — and tests whether one model wins those disagreements significantly more often, using a chi-square statistic on the discordant counts. This answers the practically important question: at the operating threshold that would be used in a diabetic care clinic, does one model make meaningfully fewer misclassifications?

3. DeLong's Test compares AUC-ROC values without fixing a decision threshold. Because both AUC estimates are computed from the same test set, they are statistically correlated. DeLong's Test accounts for this using a non-parametric, U-statistic-based estimator of the covariance between the two AUCs, making it the appropriate test for comparing overall discriminative capacity in a paired evaluation setting.

4. Why both tests are used together. The two tests are complementary. McNemar's evaluates significance at the exact operating condition that would be deployed clinically (threshold = 0.5), while DeLong's evaluates whether the models differ in their overall ability to rank at-risk versus not-at-risk feet across all thresholds. A model could show no significant difference in AUC-ROC yet show a significant difference in McNemar's Test at a specific threshold, or vice versa — each result carries a different practical implication.

5. Scope and limitation acknowledged. The target podoscope dataset (n = 300 patients) is pilot-scale, and the statistical tests are therefore treated as exploratory rather than confirmatory. Exact p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals will be reported in the final thesis. A formal written response (Rebuttal Letter dated 6 July 2026) has been submitted to the Committee Chair.
