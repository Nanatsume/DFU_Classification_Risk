C1 Revise the inclusion criteria to specify Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus patients only, since T1DM has a different pathogenesis. Reclassify "diabetes-related foot deformities" from exclusion to inclusion criteria (as IWGDF category 2 and above), and add a requirement that participants must be cognitively alert and able to follow verbal commands.

Response: We thank the Committee for this comment, which reflects important clinical distinctions. Type 1 and Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus differ in pathogenesis: Type 1 arises from autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, typically in childhood, whereas Type 2 results from progressive insulin resistance in adults. The outpatient diabetic care clinic at Buddhachinaraj Hospital predominantly serves Type 2 DM adults under routine glycaemic monitoring, and including Type 1 patients would introduce clinical heterogeneity inconsistent with this population and with the mechanism of diabetic foot risk being studied. The inclusion criteria have accordingly been narrowed to Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus. Regarding foot deformities, the Committee correctly identifies a conceptual error in the original proposal: diabetes-related structural deformities such as claw toes, hammer toes, prominent metatarsal heads, and Charcot foot are not contraindications but clinical indicators of IWGDF risk category 2 or higher, and classifying them as an exclusion criterion would systematically remove the most clinically important cases from the training set. These conditions have therefore been reclassified as an inclusion criterion. Finally, the requirement that participants be cognitively alert and able to follow verbal instructions was implicit but unspecified, and has been added to the inclusion list to ensure valid and standardised image acquisition. The revised inclusion and exclusion criteria in Section 3.2.5 of the proposal reflect all three changes.

C4 Include information on 10-gram monofilament screening and the Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI) in the Chapter 2 literature review and in the overall study design, as these are the standard clinical tools used to establish LOPS and PAD diagnoses respectively.

Response: The Committee correctly highlights that the 10-gram Semmes-Weinstein monofilament and the Ankle-Brachial Index are not peripheral background details but the clinical instruments that directly define the ground truth labels used throughout this study. Under the IWGDF framework, LOPS is diagnosed by monofilament failure at two or more standard test sites, and PAD is diagnosed when ABI falls below 0.9. Because the IWGDF risk category assigned to each foot image is derived from assessments that include these two instruments, readers must understand them to understand what the model is trained to predict. Section 2.2 of the existing proposal already describes both instruments with their procedures and diagnostic criteria. What was absent was an explicit link from these descriptions to the data collection protocol in Chapter 3, leaving the connection between the instruments and the ground truth labels unclear. A forward-reference paragraph has been added at the end of Section 2.2 making this link explicit, noting that monofilament and ABI assessments are conducted during each data collection session alongside image capture, and that the results, together with physician review of the IWGDF criteria, form the risk labels paired with each foot image.

C5 The study should move beyond binary classification and explore whether the model can distinguish patients across all four IWGDF risk categories (0, 1, 2, 3) to demonstrate the model's staging capability.

Response: We thank the Committee for this suggestion, which meaningfully extends the clinical contribution of this work. Binary classification, at-risk versus not at-risk, serves screening applications but does not distinguish the degree of risk. Clinically, the appropriate management response differs substantially across the four IWGDF categories: category 0 patients receive standard self-care education, category 1 patients require annual foot examination, category 2 patients require examination every three to six months with possible offloading or orthotic intervention, and category 3 patients require intensive monitoring every one to three months. A model capable of staging patients across these four levels would provide prognostic information that supports triaging and resource allocation in settings with limited specialist capacity. The question of whether plantar pressure images carry sufficient signal to support four-class staging is genuinely open, as some IWGDF risk factors, notably LOPS and PAD in the absence of structural deformity, may not produce a distinctive pressure signature, and we therefore treat this experiment as exploratory. The output layer has been extended to Dense(4, softmax) with categorical cross-entropy loss and inverse-frequency class weighting; performance will be reported using per-class sensitivity, macro-averaged AUC-ROC, and a confusion matrix. This extension has been incorporated into Chapter 1 Section 1.3 and Chapter 3 Section 3.5 of the revised proposal.

C6 Claiming "early detection" may overclaim the contribution of this study, since IWGDF Category 1 is defined by LOPS and PAD, which are neurological and vascular conditions not directly observable in plantar pressure images. A Category 1 patient with LOPS but no structural foot abnormality may produce no abnormal pressure pattern.

Response: The Committee's concern is acknowledged as clinically valid and important. The IWGDF risk classification system categorises diabetic foot risk on the basis of LOPS, PAD, and structural foot deformity or previous ulcer. A plantar pressure footprint image encodes mechanical load distribution across the plantar surface, which reflects structural abnormalities such as high forefoot loading and deformity-related contact patterns, but does not directly encode neurological function or vascular status. A patient with IWGDF Category 1 risk arising from LOPS alone, where neuropathy is present but the foot architecture remains structurally intact, may produce a plantar pressure image that appears completely normal, because the relevant clinical signal is absent from the modality. Claiming "early detection" of DFU risk implies the model can identify elevated risk before it is clinically apparent, which overstates the capability of a pressure-based image model. The more accurate characterisation is "risk screening," positioning the tool as identifying abnormal plantar pressure patterns associated with elevated IWGDF risk and supporting clinical referral for full assessment, rather than replacing standard monofilament and ABI examination. The framing has been revised from "early detection" to "risk screening" throughout the proposal, and a clarifying sentence has been added to Section 1.1 explicitly acknowledging this scope.

C7 Investigate whether a single model can effectively process plantar pressure data from both feet, or whether separate models should be built for each side.

Response: The Committee's concern about laterality is methodologically important. A single shared model is adopted for three reasons. First, the IWGDF framework assigns risk categories independently to each foot, and the classification task is structurally identical for both sides: the model predicts the risk category of a single plantar pressure footprint image regardless of laterality. Once foot orientation is standardised through the lateral separation and optional horizontal flip preprocessing steps, mechanical pressure patterns associated with neuropathy, structural deformity, and high-risk loading are not laterality-dependent at the feature level. Second, splitting the dataset by foot side would reduce the number of available training images from approximately 600 to 300 per model, substantially increasing the risk of overfitting on a pilot dataset of this scale. Third, patient-level risk aggregation, that is, flagging a patient as at-risk if either foot yields a positive classification, is a clinically natural extension that can be applied post-hoc regardless of the laterality design and is reserved for future work. We have added the following clarification to Section 3.3 of the revised proposal.

"A single shared model is adopted across left and right foot images for three reasons. First, the IWGDF framework assigns risk categories independently to each foot, and the classification task is structurally identical for both sides once foot orientation is standardised through lateral separation and optional horizontal flipping. Second, splitting the dataset by laterality would halve the training data from approximately 600 to 300 images per model, substantially increasing the risk of overfitting on a dataset of this scale. Third, patient-level risk aggregation, that is, flagging a patient as at-risk if either foot yields a positive classification, is a clinically natural extension that can be applied post-hoc regardless of the laterality design and is reserved for future work."

Model Design

C8 The reported feature counts for LBP and HOG appeared too few compared to the thousands of dimensions expected from raw descriptors. Clarify the feature dimensions and the selection technique applied.

Response: We thank the Committee for identifying this ambiguity, which was caused by insufficient explanation of the feature extraction methodology in the presentation. The concern is correct: raw LBP histograms may contain 256 or more bins, and raw HOG vectors may contain thousands of dimensions. Using these high-dimensional representations as input to a classifier trained on fewer than 600 images would create feature spaces that are orders of magnitude larger than the training set, substantially increasing the risk of overfitting. The baseline approach adopted from Bansal et al. (2024) therefore does not use the raw descriptor vectors. Instead, eight statistical moments are extracted from each descriptor as a compact summary of its distributional shape. For LBP, the eight features derived from the LBP co-occurrence matrix are Mean, Variance, Entropy, Skewness, Kurtosis, Energy, Contrast, and Correlation, where Contrast and Correlation are computed from the LBP co-occurrence structure and therefore differ from the GLCM Contrast and Correlation features. For HOG, the eight features are Mean, Variance, Standard Deviation, Skewness, Kurtosis, Median, Maximum, and Minimum. Together, these statistical summaries yield a 43-dimensional feature vector appropriate for a dataset of this scale. A clarifying paragraph with these details has been added to Section 3.4.1 of the revised proposal.

C9 Report cross-validation results using mean and standard deviation (mean ± SD) across folds to demonstrate model stability and to avoid over-reliance on a single fold's performance figure.

Response: The Committee correctly identifies that reporting a single performance figure per model is insufficient for assessing the reliability of cross-validation results. In five-fold cross-validation with 300 patients, each fold's test set contains approximately 60 patients, and the composition of this test set can substantially affect the observed performance metrics depending on which patients happen to be assigned to the held-out fold. A fold dominated by IWGDF category 0 patients may yield higher specificity and lower sensitivity than one dominated by category 2 or 3 patients, even when the model is identical. Reporting mean ± SD across all five folds makes fold-to-fold variability explicit, allowing assessment of whether performance is consistent across partitions or concentrated in a particularly favourable fold. This is especially important in a pilot study with a small dataset, where variability across folds is expected to be higher than in large-scale studies. The preliminary results table in Section 3.7 and the evaluation plan in Section 3.6 have been updated to report all metrics in mean ± SD format across five folds.

C10 During the examination, the Committee raised the question of whether fine-tuning is the only approach to transfer learning, or whether alternative strategies, such as training only the classifier head, gradually unfreezing layers, or applying regularisation during adaptation, could also be applied and compared.

Response: We thank the Committee for this observation, which identified an important limitation in the original proposal. The original proposal described a single two-phase strategy, Linear Probing followed by Full Fine-Tuning, as the transfer learning approach. Fine-tuning is one specific choice within a broader spectrum of adaptation strategies, each embodying a different assumption about the relationship between pre-trained features and the target domain. Full Fine-Tuning assumes the dataset is large enough to retrain all backbone parameters. Linear Probing assumes the pre-trained features are already domain-appropriate. Gradual unfreezing strategies assume lower-level features are more general than higher-level ones. Starting-point regularisation strategies assume large deviations from pre-trained weights are harmful and should be penalised. Auto-Relative Gradient Normalisation takes a data-driven approach, scaling per-layer learning rates by observed gradient-to-weight ratios during training. For a small medical imaging dataset from a specialised modality such as podoscope plantar pressure images, the optimal strategy is not known a priori, and aggressive fine-tuning may degrade performance through overfitting while a more constrained strategy is preferable. Comparing all eight strategies ensures that each backbone in RQ1 is evaluated under its best achievable adaptation approach rather than a single fixed strategy, making the backbone comparison more rigorous. The revised Chapter 3 Section 3.3 defines all eight strategies with their rationales.

C11 During the examination, the candidate was unable to clearly justify the choice of McNemar's Test and DeLong's Test as the statistical significance tests used to compare model performance. Please clarify why these two specific tests were selected, what each test evaluates, and why they are appropriate for this study.

Response: We thank the Committee for raising this point, and we provide the justification that was insufficient during the presentation. The choice of statistical tests is determined by the evaluation design: in each cross-validation fold, all compared models are evaluated on the same held-out test set, making their predictions statistically dependent. Two models evaluated on the same patients will tend to agree on easy cases and disagree on hard ones, meaning their error patterns are correlated. Applying tests designed for independent groups would ignore this correlation and produce incorrect p-values. McNemar's Test is appropriate because it operates on the 2×2 contingency table of binary predictions from both models on each test case, examining only the discordant pairs and testing whether one model wins those disagreements significantly more often under a paired null hypothesis of equal error rates. DeLong's Test is appropriate for comparing AUC-ROC values because it uses a non-parametric, U-statistic-based estimator to compute the covariance between the two AUC estimates derived from the same test set, constructing a significance test that correctly accounts for their shared patients. The two tests are complementary: McNemar's Test evaluates significance at the fixed clinical deployment threshold of 0.5, while DeLong's Test evaluates overall discriminative capacity across all thresholds. We have added the following clarification to Section 2.8.3 of the revised proposal.

"Both statistical tests are selected on the basis that all compared models are evaluated on the same held-out test set within each cross-validation fold, making their predictions statistically dependent. McNemar's Test examines the discordant pairs in the contingency table of binary predictions from two models on the same test cases, testing whether one model wins those disagreements significantly more often under a null hypothesis of equal error rates, and thereby explicitly accounts for prediction pairing without assuming independence between models. DeLong's Test accounts for the statistical dependence between two AUC-ROC estimates derived from the same test set by using a non-parametric, U-statistic-based estimator of their covariance. Together, the two tests are complementary: McNemar's Test evaluates significance at the fixed clinical deployment threshold of 0.5, while DeLong's Test evaluates overall discriminative capacity across all possible classification thresholds, following Rainio et al. (2024)."
